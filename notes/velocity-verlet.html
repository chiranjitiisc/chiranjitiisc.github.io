The Verlet family: how MD actually integrates F = ma

Molecular dynamics is, at heart, just Newton's second law solved on a computer — millions of times. The choice of how to step time forward makes or breaks everything.

Why not just use Euler?

The most obvious scheme updates positions and velocities with a straight-line guess:

x(t+Δt) = x(t) + v(t)Δt,    v(t+Δt) = v(t) + F(t)mΔt

This is the forward Euler method, and for MD it is a disaster. Its error per step is O(Δt²), but the real problem is qualitative: Euler systematically pumps energy into (or out of) the system. Simulate a planet with Euler and it spirals outward; simulate a liquid and it slowly boils itself. For a microcanonical (NVE) simulation, where constant energy is the whole point, that is fatal.

The Verlet idea

Write Taylor expansions of the position one step forward and one step backward in time:

x(t ± Δt) = x(t) ± v(t)Δt + ½a(t)Δt² ± ⅙b(t)Δt³ + O(Δt⁴)

Add the two expansions and the odd terms (velocity, third derivative) cancel:

x(t+Δt) = 2x(t) − x(t−Δt) + a(t)Δt² + O(Δt⁴)position Verlet

Two accuracy orders better than Euler, for the same single force evaluation per step — and, crucially, the scheme is time-reversible, just like Newton's equations themselves.

Velocity Verlet — the MD workhorse

Position Verlet never mentions velocities, which makes computing kinetic energy and temperature awkward. The algebraically equivalent velocity-Verlet form fixes that, and is what virtually every MD code (and every simulation on this site) uses: 

for each step:
    v = v + (dt/2) * F/m        # half kick
    x = x + dt * v              # drift
    F = compute_forces(x)       # the expensive part
    v = v + (dt/2) * F/m        # half kick

Same cost as Euler — one force call per step — but with O(Δt²) global accuracy, exact time-reversibility, and one more almost magical property: it is symplectic. It exactly conserves a "shadow" Hamiltonian close to the true one, so the energy error stays bounded, oscillating around the true value instead of drifting, even over millions of steps. This long-time stability, not raw accuracy, is why Verlet beats fancier high-order integrators (like Runge-Kutta) for MD.

Choosing the time step

The rule of thumb: Δt should be a small fraction — roughly 1/20th or less — of the shortest oscillation period in your system. For a harmonic mode of frequency ω, Verlet is only stable at all for ωΔt < 2, and accurate well below that. In practice:

  • Lennard-Jones fluids in reduced units: Δt* ≈ 0.002–0.005 (this site uses 0.004).
  • All-atom biomolecular MD: the fastest motion is a C–H bond stretch (~10 fs period), forcing Δt ≈ 1–2 fs unless those bonds are constrained.
  • Too large a step shows up as total energy drifting or exploding — try it yourself below.
