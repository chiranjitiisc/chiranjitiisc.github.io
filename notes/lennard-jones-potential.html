The Lennard-Jones potential & reduced units

One equation with two parameters captures why noble gases condense, freeze, and evaporate — and it is the fruit fly of molecular simulation.

The 12-6 potential

V(r) = 4ε [ (σ/r)12 − (σ/r)6 ]

Two competing effects, one term each:

  • −(σ/r)6 — attraction. Fluctuating electron clouds induce dipoles in neighbours; this London dispersion force between neutral atoms falls off exactly as r−6. This part is real physics.
  • +(σ/r)12 — repulsion. When electron clouds overlap, Pauli exclusion pushes atoms apart very steeply. The true form is roughly exponential; r−12 is a convenient approximation chosen mostly because it is the square of r−6 and was cheap to compute in 1924 — and still is.

The two parameters have clean geometric meanings: σ is the separation where V = 0 (an effective atomic diameter), and ε is the depth of the attractive well. The minimum sits at rmin = 21/6σ ≈ 1.122σ, where V = −ε. For argon, fitted values are σ ≈ 3.4 Å and ε/kB ≈ 120 K — which is why argon boils at 87 K: thermal energy comparable to the well depth shakes atoms out of their wells.

Reduced units: simulate once, apply everywhere

MD codes set σ = ε = m = kB = 1 and measure everything in these natural scales. One simulation then describes every Lennard-Jones substance — argon, krypton, xenon — just with different conversion factors:

QuantityReduced formFor argon, 1 unit =
Lengthr* = r/σ3.4 Å
EnergyE* = E/εkB · 120 K
TemperatureT* = kBT/ε120 K
Timet* = t / √(mσ²/ε)≈ 2.2 ps
Density (2D)ρ* = Nσ²/A—

So when the LJ fluid simulation shows T* = 0.6, an argon experimentalist reads ~72 K, and the time step Δt* = 0.004 corresponds to roughly 9 femtoseconds. Reduced units also keep numbers near 1, which is kind to floating-point arithmetic.

Cutoff and shift

V(r) never quite reaches zero, but computing all N(N−1)/2 pair interactions is wasteful when distant pairs contribute almost nothing. Standard practice: ignore pairs beyond a cutoff rc (usually 2.5σ, where V ≈ −0.016ε) and shift the potential up by V(rc) so it hits zero continuously:

Vshifted(r) = V(r) − V(rc)   for r < rc,   0 otherwise

Without the shift, particles crossing the cutoff would feel a tiny energy jump each time — thousands of such jumps add up to visible energy drift in an NVE run.

Periodic boundaries & minimum image

A few hundred particles in a box would be mostly surface. To fake bulk matter, the box is tiled with copies of itself: a particle leaving the right edge re-enters on the left (periodic boundary conditions), and each pair interacts only through the nearest of all periodic copies (the minimum-image convention). In code both are one-liners: 

x  = x - L * floor(x / L)       # wrap position into [0, L)
dx = dx - L * round(dx / L)     # nearest periodic image of a separation

For minimum image to be unambiguous the box must satisfy L ≥ 2rc.

See it live: in the LJ fluid, load the "Gas" preset and watch particles wrap across the box edges. Then quench to T* = 0.1 and watch droplets form — that's the −r−6 term at work.
