What am I looking at?

N identical atoms (mass m = 1) sit on a line, coupled to their neighbours by ideal springs (k = 1) with both ends clamped to walls. Displacements are drawn vertically so you can see them, and the equations of motion müi = k(ui+1 − 2ui + ui−1) are integrated with velocity-Verlet — the same integrator used in the Lennard-Jones simulation.

This system has exactly N independent normal modes (standing waves). Mode n has shape sin(inπ/(N+1)) and oscillates at

ωn = 2√(k/m) · sin( qna / 2 ),    qn = nπ / (N+1)a

— the phonon dispersion relation of a 1D crystal. At small q it is linear (ω ≈ cq: sound waves); near the zone boundary q = π/a it flattens (zero group velocity). The right-hand panels decompose the chain's motion into modes in real time: because the springs are harmonic, the modes never exchange energy — each bar in the spectrum stays constant while the atoms themselves move in complicated ways.

Things to try:
  • Add mode 1, then mode 5 on top — the motion looks complex, but the spectrum shows just two frozen bars: superposition at work.
  • Compare mode 2 and the highest mode: read their ω off the dispersion plot, check the periods on screen.
  • Pluck the centre — a localized kick excites many modes at once (odd ones only — why?).
  • Launch a wavepacket and watch it move at the group velocity and spread out — dispersion in action. Near the zone boundary it barely moves.
