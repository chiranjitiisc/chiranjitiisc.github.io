What am I looking at?

A gas of N identical hard disks bounces around a box. Every collision — disk-disk and disk-wall — is perfectly elastic, so the total energy never changes. Yet something dramatic happens to how that energy is shared: start every particle with exactly the same speed, and within seconds collisions redistribute the kinetic energy until the speeds follow the Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution. In 2D:

f(v) = (m v / kBT) · exp( −m v² / 2kBT )

No thermostat, no randomness is added anywhere — Newton's laws alone produce the statistics. The temperature is fixed by the (conserved) energy through equipartition: each particle carries on average kBT of kinetic energy in 2D, so kBT = KEtotal/N. The orange curve is drawn from that value alone; nothing is fitted.

Things to try:
  • Start with "All same speed" — the histogram begins as a single spike, then melts into the MB curve. This is irreversibility emerging from reversible dynamics.
  • Try "Half fast, half at rest" — watch energy flow from hot to cold particles until they share one distribution.
  • Track one particle: its speed keeps changing forever, but the distribution is stationary. Individual chaos, collective order.
  • Compare the measured ⟨v⟩ and vrms against theory (⟨v⟩ = √(πkBT/2m), vrms = √(2kBT/m) in 2D).
